Instructions for method seminar
This time, we will focus on methodological issues, and seek to identify theoretical concepts that you think
will be useful for carrying your respective analyses. In all scientific pursuits, it is of course very important to
choose a methodological approach that suits the kind of inquiry/ questions that you are engaging with, and
the kind of knowledge you seek seek to generate. It is also important that you work with concepts and
theoretical frameworks that align with your methodology, and with your epistemological outlook and
ontological position.
Hence, the task for this seminar will be to work with the following items, in an order that makes sense to
your particular method chapter (i.e., this list is not intended to provide a non-negotiable structure to your
chapters, but point to central issues to account for and reflect upon):
1) articulate in more general terms what kind of approach to conducting social science that you are relying
on, how you perceive of the knowledge generating process you are engaging in (i.e., reflect on
epistemology, the logic-of-knowledge-generation, what theory of knowledge you are relying on), and
what presumptions concerning the constitutional logic of the “world” you study that you may be adhering
to (i.e., your ontological position, the presumptions concerning the nature of being that you rely on);
2) account for what method(s) that you are using (or will use – but remember that in your final thesis
manuscripts, you will report on what the thesis is doing or what you have done in your study) to generate/
assemble empirical material or collect data, as it were;
3) motivate why you have chosen these methods – why they are suitable for your project, and what
limitations or ethical dilemmas they are associated with;
4) describe how you have deployed these methods – how your collection/generation of empirical material
has been conducted, how this methodological approach resonates, for instance, with your theoretical
outlook on knowledge (i.e., your epistemological position), and how they build up to or otherwise relate
to an analysis;
5) contemplate how you will work with the empirical material – how you will compile and/or interpret it to
generate insight and understanding, and how theory may play into this process;
6) discuss the merits and the limitations of your research design and your way of generating knowledge, and
reflect upon aspects such as generalisability, validity, reliability of the analysis you seek to forge through
your upcoming analysis (without undermining the credibility of your work);
7) identify central theoretical concepts that will be deployed in the thesis, and briefly explain how they relate
to your methodological approach;
As these pointers indicate, a methodology section is both an account for what you have done (or will
do…) and an argument whereby you seek to convince the reader that the methods you have chosen are
suitable and legitimate, given the purpose of your study and the research questions you are working with
(answering to what, how, and why this way?). It is also a place where you are expected to explain the
inherent logic of your study, and to reason around its validity and its relevance, and how your
methodological approach links to the theoretical framework you seek to deploy. With this in mind, I would
like you to write at least five A4-pages where you outline and explain you methodological approach, and
where you also look forward, towards your theory section, and link your methodological reasoning to some
theoretical framework you think will be helpful for pushing your analysis. Perhaps you spend more time on
some of the points above, than on others, depending on how far you have come with your study.
Apart from (re)reading sections of Bryman and Bell’s book on research methods, which is indeed
informative and quite traditional, I would encourage you all to read some further texts on case study research
(if you are into that), and on narrative methods (if you are in some kind of interpretative mode), to get some
inspiration and also some help in thinking through what it is that you are doing. Uploaded to Studentportalen,
you will find:
– Bent Flyvbjerg’s (2006) article “Five Misunderstandings About Case Study Research”,
which could help you conceptualise what kind of case you are actually dealing with, and how you
could discuss the issue of validity and generalisability in fun and creative ways.
– a text by Barbara Czarniawska (2000) called “The Uses of Narrative in Organization
Research” (the same text figures in a book she published in 2004). You might need to have a bit of
patience with this text, but if you should get through it, it might prove useful for thinking about how
you may set an empirical narrative apart form a more analytical one in your theses.
