### **Seminar 1 \- Literature Review**

### **1\. Introduction:** 

Digital platforms are no longer distant since they are being employed as a pivotal technology to introduce community-based sharing and organize participation, and share resources. Here, typically, when discussing exchanging, we refer to its organisation via informal relations that are based on trust and not via formal market mechanisms. It is against this backdrop that, lately, one of the most frequently used incentive mechanisms, through token-based/point-based incentive mechanisms, has been put forward as a measure to control and quantify such forms of exchange practices. There are, however, divided scholarly pursuits with this phenomenon. The current literature has discussed the components of this phenomenon in several literature streams, such as

First, the use of digital platforms for governance and coordination. This literature analyses how platforms organize interactions, construct rules, and coordinate participation between users. There is a need to comprehend how the attempts to formalise the exchange are institutionalized within the platform governance instead of neutral technical interventions.

Second, trust and social embeddedness in community-based exchange. This stream is dedicated to the role of informal relationships and social positions involved in coordination and value exchange without formal market intervention. It gives the foundation to the analysis of the possible influence of platform-mediated incentives on existing trust-based practices.

Third, reuse as socio-technical exchange systems and circular economy, this literature deals with the organisation and maintenance of reuse and redistribution by means of intermediaries, communities, and platforms. It places the case in the context of a more general discussion of circular exchange outside the sphere of the economy or market-based.

Lastly, incentive systems and motivation in digital platforms. This area explores the influence of incentives (such as token-based instruments) on participation, motivation, and behaviour in digital platforms. Assessment of incentives to encourage coordination and the risk of their destruction of trust or intrinsic motivation must be critically evaluated.

Although all streams have valuable information, each stream has different theoretical assumptions and levels of analysis, which focus on the phenomenon.

In order to counter this dissection, fields, pivotal concepts, and fundamental mechanisms are used to classify areas of research. In terms of the aspects of various mechanisms and assumptions of the authors that will guide the research toward the correct problematization, the literature review will be focused. The selection of these domains was done through intensive research and knowledge of the problem, and then converted into a narrative frame.

This literature review is aimed at mapping these streams, considering their interrelation with each other, and revealing the tensions and limitations that are still unaddressed. In this way, the review will lead to a basis for placing the current thesis into the continuous academic discussion.

**2\. Digital platforms as governance and coordination mechanisms**

This literature highlights a structured insight into the source regarding peer-to-peer (P2P) sharing platforms to address the queries. The paper (Wirtz et al., 2019\) addresses how P2P sharing platforms differ from traditional pipeline business models and seeks to identify what protects their competitive advantage. Specifically, it explores whether these platforms truly follow a "winner-takes-all" dynamic or whether their success depends on other factors such as liquidity, stakeholder trust, and loyalty (Wirtz et al., 2019, p. 464). Building on this platform-centric perspective, this paper (Blackburn et al., 2023\) relies on a meta-organizational lens, viewing circular platforms as "organisations of organisations" where authority is concentrated in the orchestrator, but activity remains with independent actors, which is key to consider.

The coordinated mechanism of platforms utilizing "highly sophisticated and dynamic supply- and demand-side pricing to equilibrate peer supply and demand, and thus manage capacity constraints more effectively than pipeline businesses" (Wirtz et al., 2019, p. 462). This pricing-based coordination is further strengthened at the organizational level, this operational capability is reinforced through governance and design choices, particularly by creating the Meta-Organizational Core by establishing the technological architecture and automating processes to allow "frictionless transactions (Blackburn et al., 2023, p. 261). Within this governance and architectural setup, the term “institutionalizing trust” is interesting as gaining trust by shifting it from personal to platform based trust such as reputation systems like reviews and ratings (Korpela, 2017, p. 56). In parallel, it also spoke about coordination mechanisms \- to stimulate interactions between separate groups, platforms provide "information and search methods," and "rules are applied to coordinate and govern these interactions" (Korpela, 2017, p. 21).

Framing the platform as the entity that "specifies the 'rules of engagement,' including the information to be provided on listings, pricing schedules, search criteria and ratings" (Wirtz et al., 2019, p. 466), which gives an understanding of how a platform can be organised. From a structural and design perspective, architecturally, platforms are "modular systems made up of a reusable platform and its complements, interaction-mediating interfaces, and access rights" (Korpela, 2017, p. 19). However, such market-based assumptions are less directly applicable in community-based settings where exchange is not primarily organised through price signals.

However, platform governance literature has its perceived risk due to the market nature of many platforms... and insecurity caused by interacting with peers who are effectively strangers (Wirtz et al., 2019, p. 468). Beyond governance-related risks, a further limitation emerges in relation to impact assessment, particularly regarding sustainability outcomes, and also its inability to measure Environmental Impact: The authors explicitly state they "could not reliably analyze or study, for instance, the negative environmental externalities caused by the platforms" (Blackburn et al., 2023, p. 276).

### **3\. Trust and social embeddedness in exchange**

Within the literature, trust is conceptualised in different ways, ranging from interpersonal trust embedded in social relations to more institutional or system-based forms of trust associated with formal rules and governance mechanisms.

The orchestrator facilitates "information sharing to improve transparency and trust" (Blackburn et al., 2023, p. 263\) and uses "automated feedback and auditing processes" to establish reliability and build internal trust (Blackburn et al., 2023, p. 263). At a more fundamental level, "without trust in actors, products, and technologies, sharing-based markets would struggle to exist" (Blackburn et al., 2025, p. 13). Accordingly, risk and uncertainty are mitigated through instilling and managing trust via institutional and legal governance mechanisms," including pre-selection of assets, rating systems, and insurance (Wirtz et al., 2019, p. 466).

Within such governance arrangements, in these environments, trust between strangers is "created and sustained through decentralized and transparent communities, resulting in reasonable and self-governed use of those shared resources" (Korpela, 2017, p. 10). Extending this logic to incentive design, "transparency within gamified systems ensures that the processes and outcomes are clear to all participants, fostering trust and engagement (Gatti et al., 2019)" (Lim et al., 2025, p. 1520).

### **4\. Circular economy and reuse as socio-technical exchange systems**

This literature addresses how reuse and redistribution are organised and sustained through intermediaries, communities, and platforms. It situates the case within broader debates on circular exchange beyond purely economic or market-based models. Within this framing, the idea of the Circular economy is formulated as a more sustainable approach to the prevailing linear economic paradigm, which is the take-make-dispose model (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 473). In parallel, here, digital innovation and gamification are also becoming more common as a means of educating and engaging people in environmental sustainability practices to support the overall Circular economy movement (Lim et al., 2025, 1515).

Much of this literature emphasises material flows, efficiency, and scaling, often conceptualising exchange in transactional or system-level terms. More specifically, this literature focuses on material flows, efficiency, and scaling, and often conceptualises exchange at the transactional or system level. Resource efficiency, waste reduction, and the creation of closed-loop systems aimed at creating value through regenerating it during production and consumption cycles are often prioritized (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 473). From this perspective, these models tend to believe that the introduction of the circular economy involves a shift in the framework of the strictly technical environmental models to the economic framework that is more inclusive (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 480).

This stream helps situate platform-mediated reuse within broader sustainability debates and highlights structural constraints such as locality and coordination complexity. In this sense, this stream foregrounds place platform-mediated reuse in the context of broader sustainability discussions and the structural limitations of locality and the complexity of coordination. Within these constraints, digital technologies are recognised as a crucial step towards faster implementation of the circular economy and scaling of community-based innovation (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 489). Complementing this view, in addition, social innovation is used to convert social problems into economic opportunities by organising local knowledge and informal networks to lower co-ordination expenses (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 490).

However, social relations and trust-based coordination are frequently treated instrumentally or implicitly, leaving unclear how circular exchange operates when embedded in community practices. In other words, social relations and coordination based on trust are often handled instrumentally or implicitly, and we do not understand how circular exchange can work in practice within community practices. As a consequence, the existing strategies are usually inefficient in systematically integrating the local values and traditional wisdom, even though they can offer the much-needed social legitimacy (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 481). Beyond this limitation, furthermore, the current literature is usually descriptive and does not provide the operational suggestions needed to apply such complicated incentive systems in specific local contexts (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 489).

**5\. Incentive systems and motivation in digital platforms**

The literature on incentive systems and gamification studies the application of extrinsic rewards to simulate participation and engagement in a digital setting (Lim et al., 2025, 1510; Mabalay, 2025, 2). These systems refer to the incorporation of the game elements into non-gaming environments to motivate participation and behaviour change using structured rewards and incentives (Lim et al., 2025, 1510).

The research outlines the opportunities and threats of incentives, such as the opportunity to motivate individuals both extrinsically and intrinsically to help achieve sustainable consumption (Lim et al., 2025, 1510). These systems are identified to have transparency that contributes to trust and engagement due to the absence of clouded processes and outcomes, as these processes and outcomes are made clear to participants (Lim et al., 2025, 1520). Moreover, the social interaction mechanisms like competition and cooperation make possible the distinctive collaborative action in the specifics of the sustainability issue (Mabalay, 2025, 5).

Notably, this literature raises concerns about issues of motivation crowding and vulnerability of incentive outcomes, especially in environments where participation is socially or intrinsically driven. Relying on Self-determination theory (SDT), researchers warn that controlled(extrinsic) motivation cannot be as effective as autonomous (intrinsic) motivation in cases when individuals perform under pressure in order to get rewards (Lim et al., 2025, 1510). Also, it is mentioned that the intention-behaviour gap exists because digital incentives can create an intention without giving it a practical, real-life implementation (Lim et al., 2025, 1532). Lastly, there is a lack of longitudinal studies to prove whether these changes in behaviour can be maintained after the novelty of the platform wears off (Mabalay, 2025, 11).

The issues are particularly acute when incentives are implemented in the context of trust-based community exchange, as platforms need to come to terms with the dilemma between formalised rewards and the informal social norms to be adopted to ensure future legitimacy (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 474).

### **6\. Synthesis and positioning**

Combined, these streams of literature can provide complementary yet incomplete insights into the formalisation of trust-based exchange using platforms and incentives. The digital platforms are positioned as a form of governance and coordination, structuring the interactions with the help of rules, pricing, architectural design, and meta-organizational coordination, and the literature on trust and social embeddedness provides the idea that the sharing-based markets cannot survive without the trust in the actors, products, and technologies (Blackburn et al., 2025, p. 13). Simultaneously, the reuse through platforms is placed in the context of wider sustainability discourses than economic or market-based models of sustainability, and incentive system literature identifies reuse as a field where gamification and rewards have been deployed to encourage participation and behaviour change in the digital space. Combined, these streams emphasise how platforms are pursuing more and more formalisation of coordination, trust, and motivation in the form of governance structures, technological architecture,e and incentive mechanisms.

In the literature, a variety of tensions occur: Formal governance versus social embeddedness: Platforms stipulate rules of engagement and institutional means like rating systems, insurance or auditing to control risk and uncertainty (Wirtz et al., 2019, p. 466), whereas trust-based coordination in community exchange is produced and reproduced by decentralized and transparent communities and informal social relations (Korpela, 2017, p. 10). Scalability and efficiency versus locality and community practices: Circular economy literature has focused on material flows and efficiency and scaling with system-level models (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 473), but structural constraints (locality, the complexity of coordination, and reliance on local knowledge and informal networks) are also discussed (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 489; 490). Incentive-based motivation versus intrinsic and trust-based participation: Gaming and incentive systems provide a chance to encourage participation, transparency, and engagement (Lim et al., 2025, 1510; 1520), but question the issue of motivation crowding, implications of incentive results, and the conflict between formalised rewards and informal social norms in a trust-based community exchange (Lim et al., 2025, 1510; Mabalay, 2025, 11; Nurrohman et al., 2025, 474).

Although it has been widely researched, the interaction between these dynamics in platforms involving early-stage, community-based interactions where trust is prior to formal government and incentive systems is not very well understood. Social relations and coordination by trust are often instrumentally or implicitly addressed, and it is not clear how circular exchange can work when it is incorporated in community practices. In turn, the current methods tend to be inefficient with regard to systematically incorporating the local values and traditional wisdom, although they may be able to provide greatly sought-after social legitimacy (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 481). 

Also, the existing literature is typically descriptive and lacks the operational recommendations to use such complex incentive systems in particular local settings (Nurrohman et al., 2025, 489).

This breach inspires the current thesis that attempts to examine these circumstances in qualitative terms using a deep case study and makes no assumptions about the efficacy or advantage of incentive-based remedies. The thesis will seek to bring empirical understanding to the intersection of platform governance, trust-based coordination, circular reuse practices, and incentive mechanisms in a given community situational context to ensure that the negotiation of trust, motivation, and coordination is achieved before full formalisation via platform governance and incentives.
