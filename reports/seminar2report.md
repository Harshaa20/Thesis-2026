# **Seminar 2 Methodology**

# **1\. Problematisation**

Digital platforms are no longer distant since they are being employed as a pivotal technology to introduce community-based sharing, organize participation, and share resources. Here, typically, when discussing exchanging, we refer to its organisation via informal relations that are based on trust and not via formal market mechanisms. It is against this backdrop that, lately, one of the most frequently used incentive mechanisms, through token-based/point-based incentive mechanisms, has been put forward as a measure to control and quantify such forms of exchange practices. There are, however, divided scholarly pursuits with this phenomenon. 

Welloop is an online application that is aimed at helping people to engage, feel mentally healthy, and be involved in youth sports communities. Sports clubs and teams use the platform to experience organized digital interactions with the young athletes, which are not within the training sessions. The platform aims not at performance measurement or competition, but at participation, wellbeing, and reflection of sports activities (Welloop AB, 2026).

Athletes use the Welloop app that allows them to engage in a brief digital experience related to the training plans. Such sessions consist of easy reflection questions posed prior to and following training, including perceived preparedness, well-being, perceived effort, and performance. The questions will help the young athletes to look back on the experience they have had in training and continue working in their sport without competing. The platform is part of the team activities of coaches or clubs, and the clubs would still have the responsibility of organising the actual training and sporting activities (Welloop AB, 2026).

The involvement in such digital reflection sessions will produce tokens on the platform. The athletes are given tokens upon the completion of the sessions as well as feedback regarding their training and well-being. The token system is, thus, founded on engagement and participation as opposed to competitive performance or athletic performance. The items that the athletes earn are now redeemable to purchase sports-related products that are delivered by external partners (Welloop AB, 2026).

The presence of this form of participation-based token system allows for investigating how digital engagement systems affect motivation, trust, and behavioural engagement in youth sports communities. Moreover, the platform context gives a context where the possibility of community-driven redistribution of sports gear with the help of a token exchange can be viewed as a potential expansion of the existing system.

According to recent studies, the conceptualisation of digital platforms as governance through algorithms implies coordination, incentives, and control embedded in non-hierarchical structures of automated mechanisms instead of being exercised by traditional hierarchical authority (Hanisch et al., 2023; Kellogg et al., 2020). These digital infrastructures influence behaviour using data-oriented guidance, appraisal, and compensatory approaches in this literature. A large part of this work, though, is concentrated on labour-platform settings, in which algorithm systems are used to replace or augment managerial control and in which governance tensions are conceptualised in terms of productivity, compliance, and resistance.

In line with this, the studies on behaviour-contingent rewards can show that extrinsic rewards can substantially influence the dynamics of participation. Although these rewards can make people more engaged, they can also displace intrinsic motivation or change the orientation of actors to normative commitment, outsourcing it to instrumental calculation (Stazyk, 2010; Wollbrant et al., 2022). However, such literature usually assesses behavioural results as opposed to considering the interpretation of such reward systems in digital-mediated governance structures.

Simultaneously, the literature on the topic of trust in online communities also indicates that engagement in online community structures is not solely dependent on formal norms but also on interpersonal and systemic trust (Mohlmann, 2016). In the experience of introducing token-based governance mechanisms in the context of trust-mediated community management, however, little has been given to the experience of such mechanisms in the context of youth, where traditional coordination has been based on informal reciprocity.

The interpretation of such mechanisms is of particular interest to understand when platforms think about expanding the token systems not only on the redemption of rewards but also on the redistribution of items to circles within themselves. Such extensions might only be legitimate and viable depending on the design, but also depending on the perceptions and negotiations of existing governance mechanisms by community actors.

This study, therefore, asks:

1. *How do participation-based digital engagement systems using token incentives influence motivation, trust, and behavioural engagement within youth sports communities?*  
2. *What do these dynamics imply for the feasibility of community-based circular exchange of sports equipment?*

## **2\. Aim / Purpose**

## This paper will investigate the extent of the experience of participation-based digital systems of digital engagement through the use of token-based incentives in the context of youth sports communities and the implications of these experiences regarding the possibility of community-based redistribution of sports equipment in circular ways. This paper discusses the example of such a digital interaction system as Welloop.

**3\. Research Design**

This paper is going to discuss a qualitative case study, as it entails a perceptive, interpretative, and feasibility study. And welloop as a case study design. The paper presents perceptions, meanings, trust, motivation, and behavioural engagement, which is why it is appropriate for a qualitative case design.

**4\. Case and Empirical Setting**

The research will be carried out in a start-up, Welloop, a sports well-being company among under-17-year-old kids situated in Uppsala. It provides tokens as a motivator to fill in the questionnaire that will be administered prior to and following the practise/training session. The earned token can be redeemed for new goods of third-party interest, such as track suits, backpacks, and socks, at present. 

The study dwells on the potential expansion of these rewards or redemption through the exchange of used sports gear as children grow so rapidly out of their sports gear (jerseys, shoes) that they will be swapped on the digital platform community. We shall be looking at the youth teams who are below the age of 17 in different sports that are played in Uppsala. 

**5\. Sampling Strategy**

With the help of the stratified purposeful approach described by Palinkas et al. (2015), the present paper will sample an information-rich case of stakeholders in various groups to offer deeper insights into digital involvement in youth sports. 

Thus, we will be purposely sampling the semi-structured interviews of 25-30 kids under the age of 17, 3-4 coaches of the respective clubs/teams, 3-4 parents of kids, and the administrators of Welloop. The participants are chosen because they are the ones who are directly engaged in the youth sports participation and digital engagement platform.

**6\. Data Collection**

The interviews will be semi-structured, and will last between 30-45 minutes, and will also be recorded on audio, transcribed, and would also be recording the answers down. The audio recording will be transcribed (Adam, 2015). All the interviews will be anonymous, and parent consent will be obtained from children under 12\. The questions will include sports involvement and encouragement, their attitudes to the digital engagement systems use, token rewards, confidence and societal standards, and their attitude to the potential sports gear.

## **7\. Interview Guide** 

To explore how participation-based digital engagement systems and token incentives are experienced within youth sports communities, semi-structured interviews will be conducted with youth athletes, coaches, and parents (Adam, 2015). The interview guide is designed to capture perspectives on sports participation, digital engagement systems, incentives, trust within sports communities, and the potential for exchanging sports equipment through a token-mediated system. The semi-structured format allows participants to elaborate on their experiences while ensuring that comparable themes are discussed across interviews.

For youth athletes, the interview begins with questions about their involvement in sports and their general experiences with training. Participants are first asked to describe their sports activities and routines through questions such as

 *“Can you tell me about the sports you play and how often you train?”* and 

*“What do you enjoy most about playing sports?”* These questions are intended to establish the participant’s relationship with sport and their motivation for participation.

The interview then explores experiences with digital engagement tools related to sports participation. Participants are asked whether they use digital applications connected to sports activities, including questions such as 

*“Do you use any apps or digital tools related to your sports training?”* Follow-up questions asked participants to reflect on their experiences with such tools and what aspects they find useful or challenging.

The next section introduced the idea of participation-based token incentives in digital platforms. Youth athletes will be asked how they would feel about earning tokens through digital engagement activities, for example, through the question 

*“On a digital platform, if you could earn tokens by answering session questions, how would you feel about earning tokens this way?”* Follow-up questions will explore whether such incentives might influence participation, including 

*“Does it make you want to participate more?”*

Subsequent questions focused on the possibility of exchanging sports equipment within the team or community. Participants will be asked about equipment sharing practices and preferences, for instance 

*“What types of sports equipment would you feel comfortable giving to teammates?”* and *“What equipment would you like to receive from others?”*

 The interview also explores practical preferences for how such exchanges might occur, through questions such as *“Would you prefer exchanging equipment at practice or matchday, or through an app?”*

The interviews also address trust and relationships within sports teams. Participants will be asked about existing practices of helping teammates, including questions such as 

*“How do you usually help teammates if someone needs equipment?”* and 

*“Do you think teammates usually trust each other when sharing equipment?”* These questions were intended to understand how trust operates within youth sports communities.

Finally, youth athletes will be asked to reflect on the broader idea of token-mediated equipment exchange within their sports community. For example, participants were asked,

 *“If there were a way to use tokens to exchange sports equipment within the team or community, what would you think about that?”*

 Follow-up questions explore whether such a system might support athletes who face financial barriers to participation.

For coaches, the interview focused on their observations of youth engagement and participation in sports. Coaches will be asked about the factors influencing continued participation, including questions such as *“What are the main reasons young athletes stop participating in sports?”* and 

*“What helps keep kids motivated to continue training?”* The interview also explores coaches’ perspectives on digital engagement systems, through questions such as 

*“What do you think about digital tools that track participation or well-being?”* and whether such tools support engagement among young athletes.

Additional questions address the role of incentives and rewards in youth sports participation. Coaches will be asked, 

*“What role do rewards or incentives play in motivating young athletes?”* and whether such mechanisms might also have unintended effects. 

The interview also examines practical aspects of sports equipment usage, including *“Which equipment do kids outgrow or replace most often?”* and whether equipment costs affect participation.

Finally, coaches will be asked to reflect on the feasibility of equipment exchange within youth sports communities through questions such as *“What do you think about the idea of a digital platform where athletes exchange equipment within the community?”* Follow-up questions explore potential challenges or concerns associated with such a system.

For parents, the interviews focus on family perspectives on youth sports participation and equipment use. Parents will be asked about their child’s engagement with sports, beginning with questions such as 

*“What motivated your child to start playing sports?”* 

The interview then explores parents’ perspectives on digital engagement systems through questions such as *“How do you feel about apps that track wellbeing or participation in sports?”*

Further questions examine parents’ views on incentive systems, including *“Do rewards or incentives help motivate children in sports?”* Follow-up questions asked whether parents have any concerns about such mechanisms. 

The interview also addresses the financial dimension of youth sports participation through questions such as *“Do sports equipment costs affect families?”* and whether equipment is sometimes shared between families or teammates.

Finally, parents will be asked to reflect on the possibility of equipment exchange within the sports community. 

For example, participants were asked, *“Where would you feel most comfortable exchanging equipment – at matchday, during practice, or through a digital platform?”* and whether such exchanges would feel safe and fair within the community.

Across all participant groups, the interview guide is designed to encourage open-ended responses and follow-up discussions. This structure allows participants to describe their experiences with sports participation, digital engagement systems, incentives, and trust within their communities, while also reflecting on the potential for token-mediated exchange of sports equipment.

## **8\. Data Analysis Plan**

The data obtained, that is, the interviews, will be transformed into transcribed texts and directly studied with the help of Thematic Analysis, one of the most efficient techniques to discover, analyze, and report a certain pattern in the qualitative data (Bell et al., 2019).

The information obtained in the semi- structured interview will be in audio recordings, which will later be transcribed into documents and edited. We will also further examine the data gathered on the assistance of tools like Ms Excel to conduct an analysis and extract the patterns of interviewees, and visualise the results with the help of Power BI. These patterns/code will subsequently be formulated on the themes which keep coming up, i.e., motivation, trust, behavioural engagement, fairness, and perspective towards redistribution.

Lastly, our literature will be connected with the analysis we have obtained.

## **9\. Limitations**

The study concentrates on circular redistribution, which has not been applied yet in that digital platform and thus the study can only comment on implications to feasibility. Also, the setting is narrowed down to youth sports communities and selected groups of interviews; further, the results would be context-sensitive and not statistically generalisable.

## **10\. Ethical Considerations**

To make the work with minors as ethical as possible, the study follows the principles based on voluntary participation and protection in the strictest way possible. Each of the youth participants makes his or her own assent as well as formal parental consent, underlining that the latter is able to withdraw at any time without any repercussions. All the data are kept anonymous to ensure that the identities of the participants remain undisclosed. Recordings and transcripts are handled in confidential processes; that is, encrypted storage and limited access are provided to preserve full confidentiality in the research process.
